The NIST AI RMF is the framework US federal agencies, financial institutions, and healthcare organisations are adopting as their standard for AI risk governance. It defines how organisations should identify, assess, and manage AI risks across the full lifecycle of an AI system.

#### Resource 2 — EU Artificial Intelligence Act

**Publisher:** European Parliament and Council of the European Union
**Length:** Full text is long — read the summary documents and Article summaries, not the full legislative text
**Access:** Free — [eur-lex.europa.eu](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32024R1689) | Readable summary: [artificialintelligenceact.eu](https://artificialintelligenceact.eu)
**Time to read:** 1 weekend (summary version)

**What it covers:**

The EU AI Act is the world's first comprehensive AI regulation, in force from August 2024 with phased compliance deadlines through 2027. Any AI system deployed in the EU — or by companies serving EU customers — falls under it. KPMG's European clients, multinational banks, and any company with EU operations must comply. This is not a future concern — it is current.

| Concept | What it means | Engineering implication |
|---------|---------------|------------------------|
| Risk classification (Unacceptable / High / Limited / Minimal) | AI systems are classified by the risk they pose to fundamental rights | You need to know what category your system falls into before deployment |
| High-risk AI systems | Systems used in healthcare, education, employment, critical infrastructure, law enforcement | These require mandatory conformity assessments, human oversight, logging, transparency |
| Transparency obligations | Users must be told they are interacting with AI | Chatbots, deepfakes, emotion recognition — specific disclosure requirements |
| Technical documentation requirements | High-risk systems must maintain documentation covering data governance, accuracy, robustness, cybersecurity | Maps directly to your SBOM, evaluation pipelines, and monitoring architecture |
| Human oversight requirements | High-risk systems must allow meaningful human intervention | Maps to your HITL (human-in-the-loop) knowledge from the LangGraph course |
| Conformity assessments | Third-party or self-assessment to prove compliance before deployment | This is the consulting engagement — KPMG helps clients do this |
| General Purpose AI (GPAI) model obligations | Foundation model providers (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google) have specific obligations | Relevant for understanding what your upstream providers are required to provide |

**Key compliance deadlines (as of 2024):**
- February 2025: Prohibited AI practices provisions apply
- August 2025: GPAI model obligations apply
- August 2026: High-risk AI system obligations fully apply
- August 2027: Remaining provisions (certain high-risk systems already on market)

**Why it matters for your target roles:** Every KPMG client with EU operations is currently scrambling to understand their obligations under the EU AI Act. The engineer who can map a client's AI system to the right risk tier, identify which technical documentation requirements apply, and specify the guardrails and logging infrastructure needed for compliance — that person is billable at a premium rate that a pure AI engineer is not.
